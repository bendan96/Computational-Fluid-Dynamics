# Executive Summary

This project presents a combined experimental and numerical investigation of the aerodynamic characteristics of a highly swept Unmanned Combat Aerial Vehicle (UCAV) configuration. The study focuses on the nonlinear aerodynamic behavior dominated by leading-edge vortices (LEVs).

**Key Objectives:**
- Measure lift, drag, and pitching moment coefficients experimentally in a low-speed wind tunnel.
- Perform steady CFD simulations using ANSYS Fluent with the k-ω SST turbulence model.
- Analyze vortex formation, development, and breakdown.
- Validate numerical results against experimental data.

**Major Findings:**
- Strong vortex lift enhances aerodynamic performance up to approximately 20° angle of attack.
- Vortex breakdown occurs beyond 22°, resulting in stall and increased induced drag.
- Experimental and numerical results show good agreement in trends, particularly in the pre-stall regime.
- Discrepancies at high angles of attack are primarily attributed to transitional flow effects and limitations of the steady RANS approach.

The study confirms that highly swept delta-wing configurations offer significant maneuverability advantages for UCAVs through vortex lift, while highlighting the challenges in accurately predicting post-stall behavior using standard turbulence models.

**Reynolds Number:** ≈ 5.3 × 10⁵  
**AoA Range:** -10° to 30°
